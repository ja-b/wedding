Getting there & around

Travel

Most of you are coming a long way — thank you for making the trip. The shape of it is simple: fly into Tokyo, then take the bullet train up to Karuizawa for the second celebration.

Before you fly: put a Suica on your phone

Our single best tip. A Suica is Japan's tap-and-go card for trains, buses, convenience stores and vending machines. iPhone users can add one straight to Apple Wallet in a couple of minutes and top it up with a US or UK card — do it before you leave home and you'll skip the ticket machines and queues on arrival. (Android has a Google Wallet Suica too.)

Flying into Tokyo Airports

  • Haneda (HND) is the closer airportAround 30–45 minutes into the city by train, bus or taxi. Choose it over Narita if your flights give you the option.
  • Narita (NRT) works tooRoughly 60–90 minutes in, via the Keisei Skyliner (to Ueno) or the Narita Express (to Tokyo / Shinjuku).
  • The Airport Limousine bus is the easy optionFrom either airport, these comfortable buses run direct to major hubs and hotels — especially handy for Shinjuku, and a breeze with luggage.
  • US & UK passports: no visa neededVisa-free for tourist stays up to 90 days — just a valid passport. No JESTA or advance form required for November 2026.

Tokyo → Karuizawa Bullet train

  • ~70 minutes, direct, no changesThe Hokuriku Shinkansen. Board wherever's easiest for where you're staying — Tokyo Station (simplest), Ueno, or Omiya. Take an Asama or Hakutaka service; the faster Kagayaki does not stop at Karuizawa.
  • Tickets are easy — reserve or just buy on the dayBook ahead for peace of mind, or buy same-day at a ticket counter or machine. Look for the green JR (Japan's national rail) signs and choose the Shinkansen to Karuizawa. Around ¥5,500–6,000 one way; covered by the Japan Rail Pass and the cheaper JR East Tokyo Wide Pass.
  • The venue is minutes from the stationA 5-minute taxi or 15-minute walk, or the hotel's free shuttle by reservation.

Where to stay

Accommodation

You'll want a base in Tokyo for the first few days, and somewhere around Karuizawa for the second celebration. We won't try to pick a Tokyo hotel for you — there are countless great ones — but here's where we'd point first-timers, all well-connected and easy to get around from.

In Tokyo Our picks

  • Akasaka-MitsukeA meeting point for several train lines, close to Nogi-jinja, with local nightlife on the doorstep.
  • Shimbashi & GinzaClassic salaryman territory — superb rail links, an easy hop to the shrine, and close to Tokyo Station for the Shinkansen.
  • ShinjukuThe city's biggest transport hub; easy access across west Tokyo and a simple route to Nogi-jinja.
  • ShibuyaAnother lively west-Tokyo hub — central and very well-connected.
  • ShimokitazawaClose to our own neighborhood (Yoyogi-Uehara), with a relaxed, characterful vibe.

In Karuizawa Our hotel picks

  • Kyukaruizawa Kikyo, Curio Collection by HiltonThe wedding venue itself — stay where it all happens, in Old Karuizawa. The loveliest, most effortless base for the day.
  • Le Grand Kyu KaruizawaA short walk from the venue in Old Karuizawa — a lovely, convenient spot right by the old main street.
  • Karuizawa Prince Hotel (West)Very comfortable, set by the station and the outlet plaza; an easy, well-run choice.
  • Hotel Indigo KaruizawaAnother very comfortable stay, near the station — and worth a look if you hold a premium travel or IHG card for the perks.
  • Holiday Inn Resort ANA Karuizawa by IHGGreat value if you're using an IHG credit (e.g. the Chase IHG card). Note it's well out of town — best saved for after the wedding.

While you're in the mountains

Around Karuizawa

A relaxed mountain town of forest walks, shopping and hot springs. A few of our favourites for the free time around the 13th and 14th.

Worth your time

  • Kyu-Karuizawa GinzaThe old main street and the heart of it all — cafés, bakeries and shops, an easy stroll from the hotel.
  • Prince Shopping PlazaOne of Japan's largest open-air outlet malls, set among the mountains and connected right to the station.

  • Harunire Terrace & Tombo-no-YuRiverside cafés among the elms at Hoshino, beside a lovely hot spring for a cold day.

Beyond the wedding

Onward adventures

You've come all this way — you may as well keep exploring. These are all within easy reach of Karuizawa, mostly a short hop by train.

Kusatsu Onsen

~1 hr 20 by bus from Karuizawa Station

Our top pick. One of Japan's greatest hot-spring towns, built around a steaming "hot water field" in its centre — buses run straight from Karuizawa Station. Well worth an overnight.

Nagano & Zenkō-ji

~30 min by bullet train

A grand 7th-century temple, one of the most important in Japan, at the heart of Nagano city.

Ski country

Hakuba · Nozawa Onsen · Myōkō · Shiga Kōgen

Some of Japan's finest snow — but in November the season is only just waking up. Most resorts don't open until December, so for reliable early snow, Hokkaido (Niseko) is the safer bet this early.

Snow Monkeys

~half day via Nagano

Wild macaques bathing in a steaming mountain hot spring at Jigokudani — unforgettable, if a bit of a journey.

Takayama & Shirakawa-go

~1.5 hrs west to Toyama, then on

Ride the bullet train west to Toyama, then carry on to Takayama's old merchant streets and the thatched farmhouses of Shirakawa-go.

Back to Tokyo

~70 min by bullet train

Round out the trip in the city — more food, more neighborhoods, more late nights.

Feeling adventurous? Renting a car is a wonderful way to roam this part of the mountains at your own pace — onsen towns, valleys and viewpoints the trains don't reach.

A note on gifts

Please — no gifts, and no money. Simply being there with us, after such a journey, is more than we could ask. Save it for your own adventures in Japan.

Packing note: Tokyo in mid-November is mild (around 10–17°C). Karuizawa sits up in the mountains and runs noticeably colder — comfortable by day, but chilly in the mornings and evenings. Bring a proper coat and warm layers for the second leg.
